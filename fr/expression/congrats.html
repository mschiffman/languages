Congratulating — « félicitations », « bravo », « chapeau »…

Congratulations is about what someone achieved — a success, an exam, a wedding, a new job. (Praising who they are — their looks or qualities — lives on the Compliment page.) Grouped from the quick “well done” to the warm, personal reaction:

Félicitations / Bravo / Chapeau  (the core set)

"Félicitations !"

“Congratulations!” — all-purpose, for achievements and happy events.


"Bravo !"

“Well done! / Bravo!” — for a performance or a success.


"Chapeau !"

“Hats off! / Nicely done!” — admiring, informal.


"Bien joué !"

“Nicely played! / Good job!”


💡 Félicitations covers achievements and life events; bravo is more for a performance or an effort you witnessed.



Tu as assuré / Tu as déchiré  (slangy, among friends)

"Tu as assuré !"

“You nailed it! / You rocked it!”


"T'as déchiré !"

“You crushed it!” — very casual.


"T'as géré !"

“You handled it brilliantly!”


"C'était top !"

“That was great!”



Félicitations pour…  (weddings, babies, new jobs)

"Félicitations pour ton mariage !"

“Congrats on your wedding!”


"Félicitations pour le bébé !"

“Congrats on the baby!”


"Félicitations pour ton nouveau boulot !"

“Congrats on the new job!”


"Tous mes vœux de bonheur." /vø/

“All my best wishes for your happiness.” — for weddings.



Je suis fier(ère) de toi  (heartfelt)

"Je suis fier(ère) de toi."

“I'm proud of you.”


"Je suis trop content(e) pour toi."

“I'm so happy for you.”


"Tu l'as bien mérité."

“You really deserved it.”


"Tu peux être fier(ère) de toi."

“You can be proud of yourself.”



À retenir

Félicitations is the all-purpose choice (and the only one you'd write on a card); bravo and chapeau react to something you saw. Keep it apart from Compliment: congratulations is for what someone did. For warmth, nothing beats je suis fier(ère) de toi or tu l'as bien mérité.
