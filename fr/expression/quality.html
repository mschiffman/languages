Expressions of quality critique in French

Click the buttons below to find the most common ways to express lukewarm quality judgments in French:

"Bof." /bɔf/

"Meh." — the quintessential French shrug of indifference.

"Mouais…" /mwɛ/

"Ehhh…" — drawn-out, skeptical, not quite convinced.

"Moyen." /mwajɛ̃/

"Average / so-so." — the blunt verdict.

"Pfff…"

A dismissive exhale — somewhere between disappointment and indifference.

"Bof bof."

"Meh meh." — doubled for extra underwhelm.



"C'est pas terrible."

"It's not great." — literally "not terrible," but used ironically to mean underwhelming.

"C'est pas fameux." /famø/

"It's nothing special." — common for food or quality.

"C'est pas top."

"It's not top-notch." — casual, understated letdown.

"C'est pas génial."

"It's not great." — mild, polite disappointment.

"C'est pas folichon." /fɔliʃɔ̃/

"It's nothing to write home about." — old-fashioned but still very much used.

"C'est pas extraordinaire. " /ɛkstʀaɔʀdinɛʀ/

"It's nothing extraordinary." — polished, slightly formal.



"Ça laisse à désirer." /dezire/

"It leaves something to be desired." — polite but pointed.

"Ça pourrait être mieux."

"It could be better." — honest and straightforward.

"Il y a mieux."

"There's better out there." — dry, slightly dismissive.

"C'est perfectible. " /pɛʀfɛktibl/

"There's room for improvement." — formal, used in feedback contexts.

"C'est améliorable. " /ameljɔrabl/

"It can be improved." — similar to perfectible, slightly more direct.



"C'est correct."

"It's okay / acceptable." — not praise, just the bare minimum of approval.

"C'est passable." /pasabl/

"It's passable / tolerable." — a reluctant pass.

"Ça va."

"It's fine." — the most versatile French non-answer.

"On fait avec."

"We'll make do." — resigned acceptance.

"Ça se mange."

"It goes down." — said of food; the lowest form of culinary praise.

"C'est acceptable. " /aksεptabl/

"It's acceptable." — neutral but damning with faint praise.
