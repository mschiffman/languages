Lesson 1

Hangul: forty letters, and you can read Korean

Every Korean syllable is one square block built from a consonant + a vowel, sometimes with a final consonant underneath. Work top to bottom on this page, playing every clip and repeating it aloud.

Consonant Vowel

How blocks stack

A vertical vowel like ㅏ sits to the right of its consonant. A horizontal vowel like ㅗ sits below it. A final consonant — called batchim — tucks underneath the whole block.

  • 가 = ㄱ + ㅏ ga — vertical vowel, so it stands to the right
  • 고 = ㄱ + ㅗ go — horizontal vowel, so it sits underneath
  • 한 = ㅎ + ㅏ + ㄴ han — final consonant (batchim) at the bottom

The 5 tense (double) consonants

Same shapes, doubled — pronounced tight and hard, with no puff of air. Squeeze your throat slightly, as if the sound is stuck for a split second before it pops out.

ㄸtttense d — 따
ㅃpptense b — 빠
ㅆsstense s — 싸

The compound vowels you'll actually meet

There are eleven combined vowels in total, but these seven do almost all the work in exam vocabulary.

ㅐaee in "bed"
ㅔesame sound as ㅐ today
ㅘwawa in "water"
ㅝwowo in "wonder"
ㅚoe"weh" — as in 회사, company
ㅟwiwe in "week"
ㅢuieu + i said quickly
Don't panic about ㅐ vs ㅔ: most Koreans pronounce them identically now. You only need to tell them apart when reading, and context does that for you.

Batchim: the consonant at the bottom

Any consonant can be written as a final, but only seven sounds are ever actually pronounced at the end of a syllable. Finals are short and unreleased — 밥 ends with your lips closed, no puff.

Final sound Example Meaning Listen
k 국 soup (guk)
n 돈 money (don)
t 옷 clothes (ot) — yes, ㅅ sounds like t here
l 물 water (mul)
m 밤 night (bam)
p 밥 rice, a meal (bap)
ng 강 river (gang)
